Our unbiased take on Mark Zuckerberg’s biased Apple Vision Pro review

CEO's Quest 3 comparison offers an interesting look at mixed-reality design trade-offs.

Kyle Orland - Feb 15, 2024 10:00 pm UTC

reader comments

255

Further Reading

Can a $3,500 headset replace your TV? We tried Vision Pro to find out
Since the launch of the Apple Vision Pro, it's not been hard to find countless thoughts and impressions on the headset from professional reviewers and random purchasers. But among all those hot takes, the opinions of Meta CEO Mark Zuckerberg stand out for a few reasons—not least of which is that he and his company have spent years of development time and lost tens of billions of dollars creating the competing Quest headset line.

For that reason alone, Zuckerberg's Instagram-posted thoughts on the Vision Pro can't be considered an impartial take on the device's pros and cons. Still, Zuckerberg's short review included its fair share of fair points, alongside some careful turns of phrase that obscure the Quest's relative deficiencies.

View this post on Instagram

A post shared by Mark Zuckerberg (@zuck)

To figure out which is which, we thought we'd consider each of the points made by Zuckerberg in his review. In doing so, we get a good viewpoint on the very different angles from which Meta and Apple are approaching mixed-reality headset design.

There’s “high-quality” and then there’s “high-quality”

Near the start of his analysis, Zuckerberg says that the "Quest 3 does high-quality passthrough with big screens, just like Vision Pro." This is only true in the most technical sense. Saying both headsets have "high-quality passthrough" is like saying an old 720p LCD TV and a new 4K OLED both have "high-quality screens."

Advertisement

Compared side by side, Apple's array of cameras and higher-resolution displays combine for a much sharper and more dynamic view of the "real world" than the Quest 3, which barely limps over the "good enough" passthrough threshold, in my experience. That display quality extends to the "big screens" Zuckerberg mentions, too, which are noticeably clearer and easier to read on the Vision Pro.

Speaking of those "big screens," the experience with 2D virtual displays is quite different in both headsets. The Vision Pro seems built from the ground up with the ability to place and resize thousands of flat iOS apps anywhere around your virtual space. Those virtual windows react to the light in the room, cast gentle shadows in your virtual view, and get occluded by objects in the real world, adding to the sense that they are really "there" with you.

The Quest, on the other hand, was built more with immersive VR experiences in mind. Yes, recent Quest OS upgrades added the ability to snap selected flat apps and system tools (e.g., the store) into place in your Quest "home environment." But the system-level "huge floating screens" experience is still much more limited than that on the Vision Pro, which offers easy free positioning and resizing of all sorts of apps. Quest users looking for something similar need to rely on a third-party tool like Virtual Desktop, which also has its own quirks and limitations.

TheWerewolf
I'll start by just saying right up front, given that the Quest is the single most popular VR headset ever, Zuck should have taken the high road and congratulated Apple for finally catching up and making a solid headset.

He could then have gone on to emphasise that the Quest and the Vision Pro seem aimed at relatively different markets and that the Quest is better aligned for its market than the AVP is and suggest that some of the AVP market can be served well with the Quest.

And then probably he should have stopped.

The Quest passthrough is adequate. It works and the AR aspects of it works well - as good as the AVP from what I've seen, but the field distortions make it uncomfortable to use in any real sense as AR other than for limited motion games.

Yes, you can read screens with it, but you need to get up close.

As for display resolution, it's the second best there is in a retail headset. Unfortunately the best is the AVP. That being said, for the vast majority of users, the difference, while noticable, isn't that huge.

The AVP has eye-tracking and this is one of those 'if you listen closely to the AVP vloggers, it's a mixed blessing' things. The Quest has 'head tracking', where you move your entire head to move the cursor to where you want to click, and weirdly, this feels more natural and less intrusive because it lets you move your eyes independently of the target and then let the actual cursor catch up. This is how most animals actually use their eyes.

In terms of hand gestures - it's about a draw from what I can see, at least at the 'desktop' level. A big problem with the Quest is that a LOT of games and apps do not support hand gestures.

That leaves ecosystems. This is where the AVP technically wins because Apple owns both halves of the equation: the headset OS and the app store. So it's much easier for them, to, if nothing else, let the AVP run iOS/iPadOS apps in windows. Given that the AVP is passthrough centric, this works.

Unfortunately, since the Quest runs Android and that's gatekept by Google, who seems unwilling to let the Quest function as a Google Play sanctioned device, Meta has some serious limitations here, made worse by their goal of gatekeeping their own store. The original idea was that the Quest was not an Android tablet you strap to your face - even though the ability to run stock Android apps is one of the most often requested features.

If Zuck decides to go to Google (or even Amazon) and get some kind of support for stock Android apps going, they could catch up fairly quickly... except for...

Desktops.

While there is a desktop Meta Quest app, its main purpose is to let you run Rift and Steam apps through the Quest as well as making the Quest the default OpenXR device on the PC,, a feature few if any vloggers have noticed is missing entirely on the AVP. However, it really is terrible as a way to connect to the Quest for even the most basic of things like moving files back and forth. Even worse, when the Quest 3 came out, Meta seriously reduced the ease of connecting your Quest to the PC as a remote storage device (which was hardly ever easy) by reducing the size of the accept connection toast and making it vanish quickly. There should be a setting in the settings to always accept USB connections.

In the end though, the one 'better' feature is very hard to argue against and that's price. The Quest IS ¹/₇th the price of an AVP and it does deliver more than 80% of the functionality. That means that if you and up to six of your friends want to sit together in a virtual movie theatre and can handle a small loss in video quality - you can buy SEVEN Quest 3s and hand them out to friends on Movie Night for the same price as one AVP, and not leaving you sitting alone, well manicured and coiffed in your upscale living room in designer clothes staring into space for the evening.

Kendokaa
I'm a camera and VR/AR/HMD nerd and I have some opinions about Apple going with camera based AR and how they will improve it:

Waveguide display based AR like the Hololens is kind of a dead end at the moment. The field of view is still tiny and the displays aren't very bright and the improvements are very slow to come. Apple instead went with camera based AR which at least has a path to getting better. Getting better on the camera side means having a higher dynamic range and that means having less noise. The path seems mostly dependant on getting better and better at computational videography and possibly AI image reconstruction. Camera sensors have stopped getting much better a long time ago, with tiny gains in large sensors and mediocre gains in small sensors. All changes to the camera sensors, lenses and settings have tradeoffs:

-For lower noise, make the sensor larger at the same resolution. The pixels get larger and more sensitive. This requires a larger and heavier lens

-For lower noise, make the pixels larger. This lowers the resolution. If Apple wanted to improve the resolution without AI super resolution, there would be more noise

-For a brighter image which leads to lower noise, make the lens aperture larger (smaller F number). The upper limit of what's reasonably possible isn't that much better than what Apple went with (F2.0). Depending on sensor size, you also get less and less of the scene in focus because the depth of field shrinks as the sensor size increases at the same aperture. Eye tracked autofocus could help but I think it'd be noticeable that only a tiny bit of your vision is crisp

The only thing left is to process the camera feeds more, better and in real time. There's a limit to how good denoising can get because you can't pull a signal from pure noise but there are still improvements to be made.

Note:
I don't know the size of the sensors in the Vision Pro and unfortunately Apple only mentions the 35mm equivalent focal length (18mm) and 6.5MP resolution so I can only guess. I think they went with something as big or bigger than a smartphone sensor because smartphone camera modules are tiny and weigh nothing. Surely they were able to fit something a bit heavier in the 600g headset. Definitely not as big as a Micro Four Thirds sensor (17.3mm wide) though, the lenses would be too big and heavy.
